1. Executive Summary

Overview of the Topic

Quieter Oceans, an industry-partnerships initiative of Wave, is a strong strategic opportunity that links commercial shipping performance to ocean regeneration. Human activity generates a significant amount of noise in the seascape, with negative effects for many marine mammals, fish, and invertebrates. Commercial shipping is a leading source of this noise and is the focus of the initiative.

The initiative ties shipping performance to ocean regeneration because many of the same design and operational measures that reduce underwater radiated noise (URN) also improve fuel efficiency and reduce greenhouse gas emissions. This creates a commercially relevant pathway through which ship designers and operators can generate private value while reducing ecological disturbance and contributing to broader social and environmental outcomes.

Headline Value Proposition

The value proposition is multi-stakeholder. Shipping companies can improve fuel efficiency, support operating performance, strengthen compliance readiness, and reinforce environmental positioning. At the same time, marine ecosystems, local communities, and global society also stand to benefit. For marine ecosystems, URN reduction lowers acoustic disturbance that affects species communication, foraging, and habitat use. For coastal and blue-economy stakeholders, quieter shipping may support stronger tourism and fisheries outcomes where sensitive species and commercial activity overlap. For society more broadly, the same interventions contribute to greenhouse gas reduction and associated climate benefits. Taken together, Quieter Oceans is more than a biodiversity issue; it is a commercially viable route to ocean regeneration.

The opportunity for value creation is broad, broader than can readily be captured in a single impact analysis. Phase 1 of Ecotone's work, summarized in this report, therefore does two things: it establishes the wider value proposition of URN reduction in commercial shipping, and it defines the scope of a targeted Phase 2 Social Return on Investment (SROI) analysis.

Key Insights

Three conclusions from Phase 1 shape the SROI scope:

  • The business case is strongest when URN reduction is positioned as part of the decarbonization and vessel-efficiency agenda rather than as a stand-alone environmental add-on. This restricts the breadth of relevant URN-reducing investments that should be considered.
  • The analysis should focus on measures aligned with quiet-ship notation, a globally recognized certification that frames noise performance as a management variable.
  • The ecological component must remain region-specific, while the financial and climate components can be treated as broadly transferable across fleets and geographies.

Defined SROI Scope (High-Level)

Following a detailed review of available evidence, the Phase 2 SROI will focus on three modules of benefits: (1) global fuel-efficiency, operator ROI, and greenhouse gas co-benefits; (2) ecological outcomes in the Salish Sea, including whale-related tourism, fisheries-related pathways, and selected ecosystem-value proxies where defensible; and (3) supplemental regional case studies, such as the Baltic Sea and Mediterranean Sea, used to strengthen the broader business case without expanding the core quantitative model.

The core SROI will be limited to newbuild container ships, bulk carriers, and oil tankers adopting quiet-ship-notation-aligned design and operational measures. It will monetize only those outcomes that can be valued with sufficient rigor, and will treat other strategically important outcomes as quantified directional indicators or narrative-only value drivers.

Key Risks and Uncertainties

The main analytical constraint is ecological monetization. Direct valuation of URN-related ecological impacts remains limited by the available evidence and by the need to connect acoustic change to measurable economic outcomes through multiple causal steps. Transferability across ecosystems is also uncertain. For that reason, supplemental regional examples should remain outside the core SROI ratio and serve instead as supporting proof points.

The key insight of Phase 1 is clear: quieter shipping has the potential to create measurable private, social, and environmental value for multiple stakeholders. The strongest analytical case comes from pairing a globally relevant operator and climate case with a tightly bounded, region-specific ecological proof point. That is the role of Phase 2.

2. Topic Overview & Strategic Context

Topic Definition

Quieter Oceans is an initiative of Wave that, through the development of industry partnerships, is designed to reduce underwater radiated noise (URN) from commercial shipping and transportation through a defined set of design and operational interventions. The emphasis is on URN-reduction measures aligned with greenhouse gas mitigation, reflecting the fact that many of the same underlying technical drivers, particularly cavitation, propulsion inefficiency, and operating profile, shape both underwater noise and emissions performance.

URN from commercial shipping has increased over time and is now widely recognized as a material marine-environment issue. Anthropogenic ocean noise has intensified significantly in the industrial era, with low-frequency noise in some areas reported to have risen several-fold since the mid-20th century, much of it attributed to growing commercial shipping activity (Duarte et al., 2021; NOAA PMEL, 2014). This matters because sound is a primary sensory channel for many marine species, and the International Maritime Organization now identifies commercial shipping as one of the main contributors to URN, with documented effects on critical life functions across marine mammals, fish, and invertebrates (IMO, 2024). The scale of the problem is widespread, chronic, and embedded in mainstream shipping operations rather than confined to isolated or niche contexts.

Commercial Shipping and the URN Reduction Opportunity

URN is an inherent byproduct of commercial shipping. In practical terms it is generated primarily through propeller cavitation, hull-propeller interaction, onboard machinery vibration, and the hydrodynamic behavior of the vessel as it moves through the water. In large commercial vessels these sources combine to create persistent low-frequency noise that can travel over long distances and materially alter the acoustic environment used by marine species for communication, navigation, prey detection, and predator avoidance.

Policy Landscape and State of Adoption

The policy landscape for URN from commercial shipping is still emerging and largely non-mandatory, but it is becoming more structured and visible. At the global level, the International Maritime Organization (IMO) adopted revised Guidelines for the Reduction of Underwater Radiated Noise from Shipping in 2023 and is currently running an experience-building phase through 2026, alongside an action plan intended to address barriers to uptake and implementation (IMO, 2023, 2024).

Regionally, attention is more advanced in a small number of geographies. In the Baltic, HELCOM is actively tracking underwater noise from shipping and integrating the issue into its regional marine-environment agenda, while in Canada and the U.S. voluntary slowdown and quiet-vessel initiatives have created one of the most developed real-world testing environments for URN reduction (HELCOM, 2025; NOAA Fisheries, 2024). Overall, URN is moving from a niche environmental topic toward a recognized component of shipping's broader environmental footprint.

Adoption across the industry, however, remains early-stage and uneven. Most measures are still voluntary, and the market is characterized more by pilot activity, port incentives, route-specific slowdowns, and quiet-ship notation systems than by broad-based mandatory adoption. Most major classification societies now offer some form of quiet-ship notation (see Appendix C), which suggests the technical and verification architecture is developing, but uptake remains limited relative to the size of the global fleet (Smith & Rigby, 2022; IMO, 2023; DNV, 2021).

3. Intervention Landscape

Because URN in commercial shipping has multiple sources, reducing it does not depend on a single technology or operating change. It requires a portfolio of interventions that affect how noise is generated, transmitted, and experienced across different vessel classes and operating contexts. The intervention landscape for Quieter Oceans should therefore be understood as a set of commercially relevant levers rather than a single technical solution.

At a high level, the most relevant interventions fall into two broad categories. Design interventions reduce noise by improving the physical and hydrodynamic characteristics of the vessel. Operational interventions influence how the vessel is used and maintained, affecting both source-level noise and exposure in sensitive areas.

Intervention Categories

The design-side landscape centers on four bundles: propeller, wake, and cavitation management; hull design and coatings; machinery isolation and structural damping; and wind-assisted propulsion. On the operational side, the most relevant bundles are speed reduction, maintenance, and other operating measures; and routing and temporal management.

4. Evidence Review & Key Insights

A detailed evidence review is essential to determine the strength of the causal linkages that connect interventions to outcomes, and to assess the likelihood of being able to monetize long-term outcomes. The findings below inform both the logic model presented in Section 8 and the scoping decisions in Section 6.

Summary of Evidence

Evidence is strongest for the proposition that URN is a material ecological pressure and that marine mammals and fish are particularly vulnerable to masking, physiological stress, and behavioral disruption (Duarte et al., 2021; Merchant, 2019; Williams et al., 2015). The literature also provides substantial support for the technical efficacy of mitigation measures. Engineering controls such as propeller optimization and operational measures such as speed reduction have shown moderate-to-high efficacy in lowering source-level noise and, in many cases, also align with energy-efficiency objectives (Leaper, 2019; Leaper & Renilson, 2021; Smith & Rigby, 2022; VARD, 2023).

Evidence Strength and Gaps

Evidence is more moderate when the question shifts from ecological disturbance to monetized economic impacts. There is a plausible and partially documented basis for linking URN reduction to ecotourism value, fisheries productivity, and selected avoided-loss pathways, but those relationships are not uniformly robust across all contexts (Merchant, 2019; Vakili et al., 2021; Smith & Rigby, 2022). The main limitation is not whether acoustic disturbance matters ecologically, but whether available studies support a sufficiently direct and defensible translation into monetizable outcomes.

Evidence is weakest where valuation must be transferred across ecosystems. Species composition, habitat structure, baseline sound conditions, and shipping patterns vary significantly across regions. As a result, even where a strong ecological signal is observed in one location, the magnitude of that effect cannot automatically be assumed to hold in another (Duarte et al., 2021; Erbe et al., 2019; Sole et al., 2023).

A further insight is that the acoustic profile of the global fleet is highly concentrated. A relatively small share of vessels accounts for a disproportionate share of total radiated noise energy, suggesting that targeted intervention on commercially important and acoustically significant vessel classes may generate outsized returns (Duarte et al., 2021; Frisk, 2012; Smith & Rigby, 2022).

Key Insights

The evidence supports a confident, broad value-proposition narrative paired with a disciplined, selective SROI. Technical efficacy and the link between efficiency measures and fuel and GHG outcomes are well established and globally transferable. Ecological response is well supported in principle, but its translation into monetary value is strongest where the causal chain is short, the species response is well documented, and regional data are available. These conditions point toward anchoring the ecological case in a single high-evidence geography rather than aggregating across regions.

5. Value Creation Across the Topic

Quieter Oceans creates value through a combination of private financial returns, broader social benefits, and ecological improvement. This is one of the topic's central strengths: the business case for quieter shipping is not limited to environmental stewardship.

Tourism and Fisheries as Value Translators

Tourism is especially important within this framework because it can translate ecological improvement into a more monetizable form of value. Where ship noise affects species that matter to visitor experience and regional identity, quieter shipping can support stronger tourism demand, visitor satisfaction, or willingness-to-pay. Fisheries can play a similar role where improved acoustic conditions contribute to more stable biological outcomes for commercially important species, though this pathway requires care. In predator-prey systems where a quieter ocean also sharpens the hunting efficiency of acoustically sensitive predators — for example, killer whales preying on salmon — reduced noise could increase prey mortality rather than reduce it. The net effect on fisheries is therefore context-dependent and, in some settings, ambiguous in sign, and should be modeled accordingly rather than assumed positive.

6. Prioritization & SROI Scope Definition

This section defines the boundaries of the Phase 2 SROI. While Quieter Oceans has broad strategic relevance, the core model must remain limited to components that are both strategically material and sufficiently robust for disciplined valuation. The scope was shaped through a sequence of explicit scoping decisions, each intended to strengthen analytical credibility while preserving commercial relevance.

Scope Decision: Focus on Newbuilds

The first scoping decision is to focus the core SROI on newbuild vessels rather than retrofits. Incremental costs of URN- and GHG-aligned measures are more observable and comparable at the design stage, whereas retrofit costs are highly heterogeneous. The newbuild focus is also aligned with the long-lived nature of shipping assets: design choices influence vessel performance over decades.

One trade-off this focus introduces is displacement risk: concentrating on newbuilds does not address the existing fleet, and without incentives to retrofit or retire the noisiest in-service vessels, owners may sell them into secondary markets rather than scrap them — shifting noise to other regions instead of removing it, a particular environmental-justice concern for the Global South. Phase 2 should therefore treat newbuild gains as a net improvement only where they are not offset by displaced legacy vessels, and should flag retirement or retrofit of the noisiest existing ships as a complementary priority.

Scope Decision: Bulk Carriers, Oil Tankers, and Container Ships

The second decision is to focus on bulk carriers, oil tankers, and container ships. Together they account for roughly 85% of global fleet capacity by deadweight tonnage (bulk carriers 42.5%, oil tankers 27.5%, container ships 14.9%) and about 30% of ships by number. The acoustic evidence reinforces this further: analysis of 1,582 vessels transiting Haro Strait in the Salish Sea found that half of all acoustic power radiated by the fleet originates from just 15% of ships, and that container ships carry the highest average source levels (178 ± 4 dB) of any vessel class, with 43% qualifying as gross acoustic polluters — by far the highest proportion of any class. Cargo and container ships together account for more than two-thirds of these gross polluters (Veirs et al., 2018). Targeting these classes is therefore simultaneously the most commercially and acoustically efficient strategy.

Scope Decision: Quiet-Ship-Notation-Aligned Measures

The third decision is to focus on design and operational measures aligned with the attainment of quiet-ship notation. Ecotone developed an internal three-tier crosswalk to harmonize notation systems across class societies for analytical purposes. The analysis focuses on Tier B — notation levels that represent a meaningful but still commercially feasible degree of quieting for mainstream vessels (see Appendix C).

Scope Decision: Region-Specific Ecological Case

A globally generalized ecological SROI was not selected because ecological outcomes are inherently exposure- and ecosystem-specific. The regional assessment focused on the factors most relevant to ecological valuation through a two-stage screening process.

Value Distribution

Value is distributed unevenly, and that unevenness is precisely what makes an SROI framework useful. Shipping operators realize the most immediate and commercially legible returns — through fuel performance, reduced maintenance, and regulatory readiness — and are the clearest candidates for monetized pathways. Marine ecosystems experience direct ecological improvements that require anthropogenic value translators such as tourism and fisheries before they can enter a quantitative model. Coastal communities realize more localized benefits that are monetizable only where the causal chain is sufficiently robust and data-rich. Global society benefits through avoided climate damages and biodiversity support, addressed through social cost of carbon monetization and narrative framing. This gradient from directly monetizable to narrative-only is what drives the report's tiered approach to valuation — not a limitation of the analysis, but a discipline built into its design.

8. Logic Model for the Topic

The logic model is the primary architectural backbone of the SROI analysis. It maps the causal chain, deconstructing the system into verifiable, sequential stages that bridge engineering changes, physical acoustic outputs, biological responses, and finally monetizable economic and social impacts. It performs three functions:

  • Tracing activities to outputs: design optimization bundles (e.g., low-cavitation propellers) and operational bundles (e.g., speed reduction) are traced to immediate physical outputs such as low-noise certified vessels and reduced operational RPM.
  • Mapping stakeholder value: outcomes are disaggregated by stakeholder group — shipping companies, marine ecosystems, local communities, and global society — to capture all dimensions of private and public value creation.
  • Identifying monetization nodes: following outcomes from short-term to long-term isolates the nodes where financial proxies can be defensibly applied, such as linking reduced acoustic masking to improved reproductive success of whales and, in turn, to increased whale-watching tourism revenue.

Key Causal Pathways

1. The marine ecotourism pathway. Anthropogenic acoustic disturbance leads to acoustic masking, which disrupts foraging and prey detection, reducing caloric intake, impairing reproductive success, and stalling population recovery — ultimately threatening ecotourism revenue and existence value.
2. The fisheries productivity pathway. Shipping noise induces physiological stress in key species (e.g., salmon), altering schooling and survival probabilities, reducing adult abundance, and lowering catch per unit effort. This pathway is directionally positive only where noise is a net stressor on the target species; in predator-prey systems where a quieter environment also benefits an acoustically superior predator, the net fisheries effect may be reduced or reversed.
3. The synergistic decarbonization and private-ROI pathway. Suboptimal propulsion and high operating speeds increase cavitation and fuel burn, elevating both URN and GHG emissions and raising fuel OPEX and the social cost of carbon. The same measures that quiet the vessel reduce these costs.
4. The vessel-strike pathway — a trade-off to model explicitly. Lower vessel speeds reduce the lethality of any collision but increase time spent in transit and therefore the probability of a vessel-animal interaction. The net change in strike mortality should be modeled directly rather than assumed to fall.
Noise reduction as a force multiplier. For endangered populations such as Southern Resident killer whales, noise reduction on its own is not sufficient to restore population growth; recovery also depends on complementary measures such as salmon recovery, dam removal, and fishing management. Noise reduction's value is as a force multiplier and an immediate intervention: quieter water benefits animals instantly, buying time for slower-acting recovery efforts to take hold.

9. SROI Design Foundations

With the scope defined, Phase 2 will be structured as a three-part analytical model. This section sets out the design logic, priority metrics, core data requirements, and principal uncertainties for each component.

Overall Analytical Architecture

The recommended model has three parts: a global financial and climate module, a core ecological module, and supplemental regional case studies. Only the first two feed into the core SROI ratio. The model therefore combines a globally relevant commercial case with a region-specific ecological proof point.

This module quantifies benefits that are broadly transferable across fleets and geographies: avoided fuel costs, operator ROI, payback period, and avoided greenhouse gas emissions. This module should form the commercial backbone of the SROI.

Supplemental case studies strengthen the broader business case and demonstrate that the relevance of Quieter Oceans extends beyond the core ecological model. Their purpose is strategic rather than structural, and they should not be incorporated into the aggregate SROI ratio.

Key Outputs

  • Disaggregated ROI by vessel class
  • Total operator financial return under the modeled adoption scenario
  • Avoided greenhouse gas emissions and associated social value
  • Selected ecological monetized outcomes in the core geography (Salish Sea)
  • An overall SROI ratio
  • Quantified-only and narrative-only outcomes reported separately

Data Requirements and Gaps

The model will require three primary categories of inputs: ship and cost data (vessel specifications, intervention costs, fuel-use and adoption assumptions); acoustic and biological data (URN exposure, marine-life response, relevant ecological indicators); and valuation data (tourism, fisheries, climate, and selected ecosystem-value proxies). The main constraint remains ecological monetization.

Assumptions and Sensitivity Testing

Phase 2 will include sensitivity testing around intervention costs, fuel savings, adoption rates, carbon values, and ecological effect sizes. The principal uncertainties are likely to arise from intervention effectiveness at reducing URN, ecological effect size, valuation proxy selection, benefits transfer, and persistence of operational measures over time.

Implication for Phase 2 SROI

The role of Phase 2 is not to revisit scope. It is to quantify the most defensible value pathways within the boundaries established in Section 6. A well-structured model will allow Wave to communicate both the private and public value of quieter shipping without overextending beyond the available evidence.

10. Strategic Implications & Business Case

Strategic Importance for Wave

Quieter Oceans gives Wave a concrete way to connect ocean regeneration to a commercially legible investment case. It strengthens Wave's positioning with operators, investors, and ecosystem stakeholders by showing where value is created, who captures it, and which outcomes can be defended quantitatively. The strategic value of the topic lies in its ability to bridge agendas that are often treated separately: for operators it can be framed in terms of vessel efficiency, fuel savings, compliance readiness, and environmental positioning; for ecosystem stakeholders, in terms of reduced acoustic disturbance, healthier marine species, and stronger place-based ecological outcomes.

Preliminary Business Case

The business case for quieter shipping is strongest when positioned as a performance and stewardship agenda rather than as a stand-alone biodiversity issue. Some of the main drivers of URN — especially propeller cavitation, hull-propeller interaction, and inefficient operations — are also key drivers of fuel inefficiency and greenhouse gas emissions. As a result, measures such as propeller optimization, hull improvements, maintenance, and speed optimization can often reduce fuel use, lower emissions, and reduce low-frequency URN at the same time (IMO, 2023; Transport Canada, 2023).

Evidence from voluntary slowdown trials in the Salish Sea found that slower vessel speeds reduced underwater noise and were associated with a modeled 22% reduction in potential lost foraging time for Southern Resident killer whales in the trial area (Joy et al., 2019).
